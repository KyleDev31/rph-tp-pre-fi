The Constitution During The American Era

The American period in Philippine history, spanning from 1899 to 1935, marked a crucial era of transition for the archipelago as it shifted from Spanish colonial rule to American governance. One of the key aspects of this transformation was the development and evolution of the Philippine Constitution. This article delves into the historical background, drafting processes, and significant provisions of the constitutions during this period. The United States acquired the Philippines from Spain through the Treaty of Paris, signed on December 10, 1898, concluding the Spanish-American War. This marked the beginning of American influence in the Philippines. Prior to the American colonization, the First Philippine Republic was established on January 23, 1899, with Emilio Aguinaldo as its president. However, the Philippine-American War erupted, leading to the defeat of the First Republic. During the transitional period after the defeat of the First Republic, the Philippines was placed under American military rule. This period saw the establishment of several municipal and provincial governments, laying the groundwork for a more formalized governance structure.

The Spooner Amendment of 1901 marked a significant shift towards American control as it granted the U.S. President the authority to appoint the governor-general and members of the Philippine Commission. This period witnessed the formulation of laws and policies that influenced the eventual constitutional developments. The constitutional landscape continued to evolve with the enactment of the Philippine Organic Act in 1902, also known as the Philippine Bill of 1902. It served as the de facto constitution during the early American period, establishing a bicameral legislature appointed by the U.S. President and providing for a bill of rights that institutionalized certain civil liberties for the Filipino people. The Jones Act of 1916 represented a milestone in granting Filipinos greater participation in governance. It established a bicameral legislature with an elected lower house and further emphasized the protection of civil liberties. The Tydings-McDuffie Act of 1934 paved the way for Philippine independence by 1946, leading to the drafting of a new constitution and the establishment of the Commonwealth of the Philippines. The 1935 Constitution was drafted by a constitutional convention convened in 1934 and ratified by the Filipino people on May 14, 1935. It went into effect on November 15, 1935, marking the establishment of the Commonwealth of the Philippines. This constitution established a democratic government with a presidential system, enshrined the Bill of Rights, and affirmed the sovereignty of the Filipino people, setting the groundwork for full independence, which was realized in 1946. The constitutions during this era laid the foundation for the democratic principles that continue to shape the governance of the Philippines to this day.
